## Results

### Results
Table 1 reports overall performance on PipeRCA-Bench. The strongest simple structural baseline, `centrality`, achieves Top-1 `0.2911`, MRR `0.5725`, and an average of `1.2800` assets inspected before the true cause. The strongest evidence-only baseline, `quality_only`, achieves Top-1 `0.3089`, MRR `0.5584`, and `1.4156` assets inspected before the true cause. In contrast, **LineageRank-H** reaches Top-1 `0.6178`, MRR `0.7907`, and reduces the average inspection burden to `0.4956`. The learned variant, **LineageRank-L**, reaches Top-1 `0.8911`, MRR `0.9395`, and reduces the average inspection burden further to `0.1667`.

These results support three main conclusions. First, pipeline RCA is not solved by simple proximity heuristics: both runtime-only and design-only distance baselines perform poorly. Second, evidence-only approaches are also insufficient; while recent changes, freshness, and local test failures are informative, they do not consistently identify the true upstream culprit by themselves. Third, the fused approach substantially outperforms both structural and evidence-only baselines, indicating that modern metadata ecosystems expose complementary signals that can be combined effectively for RCA.

The confidence interval analysis in Table 2 shows that the primary gains are not marginal. For example, the Top-1 95% confidence interval for LineageRank-H is `[0.5733, 0.6622]`, while the corresponding intervals for `centrality` and `quality_only` are `[0.2511, 0.3356]` and `[0.2667, 0.3489]`, respectively. The paired bootstrap analysis in Table 3 further confirms these improvements. Relative to `centrality`, LineageRank-H improves Top-1 by `0.3267` and MRR by `0.2183`, while reducing the number of wrongly inspected assets before the true cause by `0.7844`. Relative to `quality_only`, the corresponding improvements are similarly large. In all three metrics, the paired bootstrap tests yield rounded `p = 0.0000` under the current resampling setup.

The learned variant improves substantially over the heuristic version, but the audit in Table 4 helps interpret this gain. Removing the fault-prior feature only reduces learned Top-1 from `0.8911` to `0.8689`, suggesting that the model is not succeeding merely because of one hand-engineered prior. At the same time, the ablations reveal that evidence features are especially strong in the current benchmark distribution: the evidence-only learned variant reaches Top-1 `0.8489`, compared with `0.6356` for the structure-only variant. This observation is important for interpretation and motivates a more cautious positioning of the learned model as a strong upper-bound system rather than a fully settled operational answer.

Finally, Table 7 provides a small but important realism validation. On a real public-data taxi pipeline built from official NYC TLC January 2024 data and the official taxi zone lookup, LineageRank-H achieves perfect Top-1 accuracy across 30 rerun-based faulted incidents, compared with `0.8000` for `centrality` and `0.4000` for `quality_only`. We do not treat this case study as the primary benchmark because it is smaller and easier than PipeRCA-Bench. Instead, we use it to show that the fused RCA framing can be exercised on actual reruns with captured runtime lineage events rather than only on synthetic incident manifests.

## Threats To Validity

### Threats to Validity
The first threat is **incident realism**. PipeRCA-Bench is a controlled benchmark with generated incidents rather than a corpus of organically occurring production failures. This can bias both signal distributions and fault frequency. We mitigate this in three ways. First, we ground the fault taxonomy in prior empirical and systems work on data-pipeline quality, schema evolution, and staleness. Second, we include multiple pipeline families with different structures. Third, we add a real public-data validation layer based on actual reruns of a taxi ETL pipeline with captured runtime lineage edges. Even so, we do not claim that PipeRCA-Bench fully represents enterprise incident diversity.

The second threat is **benchmark bias toward evidence features**. The leakage and ablation audit shows that evidence-only learning performs strongly, which means the current benchmark rewards quality and change signals more than graph structure alone. This does not invalidate the benchmark, because evidence signals are genuinely important in practice, but it does mean that the learned variant should be interpreted with caution. We therefore emphasize the heuristic LineageRank-H results as the main operational contribution and present LineageRank-L as an upper-bound learned variant.

The third threat is **external validity across tool ecosystems**. The benchmark is implemented with OSS-first assumptions around Airflow, dbt, OpenLineage-style lineage, and DuckDB/PostgreSQL-scale execution. These choices are appropriate for reproducibility and align with the paper’s target problem setting, but they do not imply immediate transfer to all production platforms, warehouses, or observability stacks.

The fourth threat is **ground-truth simplification**. In the benchmark, each incident has a designated primary root cause. Real incidents may involve multiple interacting upstream causes, cascading failures, or ambiguous causal boundaries. We treat the current task as a ranked single-primary-cause benchmark because it yields a tractable and reproducible starting point for empirical comparison.

The fifth threat is **case-study scale**. The real public-data taxi validation is smaller and structurally easier than PipeRCA-Bench. Its role in the paper is therefore limited: it supports realism and rerun-based feasibility, but it should not be overinterpreted as a replacement for the main benchmark.

## Novelty And Positioning

### Novelty and Positioning
The novelty of this paper does not lie in the general idea that lineage helps debugging. Provenance and lineage have long been used to support inspection, explanation, screening, and dashboard-driven tracing. Our contribution is narrower and more specific: we formulate **root-cause analysis in modern OSS data pipelines as a ranked retrieval problem**, construct a **reproducible benchmark** for that task, and show that **fusing runtime lineage, design lineage, and operational evidence under partial observability** materially improves culprit prioritization.

This positioning distinguishes our work from three adjacent lines of prior work. First, provenance capture papers focus on collecting and querying derivation metadata, but do not define a benchmarked RCA ranking task for ETL/ELT incidents. Second, observability tools and incident dashboards help practitioners view failures and browse metadata, but they do not provide a systematic, reusable evaluation of culprit ranking methods. Third, RCA benchmarks in adjacent domains such as microservices establish the value of benchmark-driven diagnosis, but they operate over different graph semantics and evidence types than those found in data engineering pipelines.

The paper’s strongest novelty claim is therefore a **combination claim**: to the best of our knowledge, existing literature and OSS systems provide lineage capture, provenance screening, and incident workflows, but not an open benchmark and ranking framework for upstream culprit prioritization in data pipelines under incomplete observability. This claim is stronger and safer than simply asserting “the first lineage-aware RCA system.”

We also position the two method variants deliberately. **LineageRank-H** is the main contribution because it is interpretable, operationally plausible, and empirically strong. **LineageRank-L** demonstrates that lightweight learning over the same features provides a substantially stronger upper bound, but we avoid claiming that it is the only or necessarily best deployment choice. This separation helps keep the paper grounded as systems-and-methodology research rather than drifting into overclaimed AI framing.

## Intro Novelty Paragraph

### Conservative novelty paragraph for the introduction
Existing work has substantially improved lineage and provenance capture for data workflows, while modern observability stacks expose tests, contracts, freshness signals, and incident workflows. However, these advances still leave a gap between metadata availability and operational diagnosis: practitioners can often see what failed and what depends on what, but still lack a principled answer to which upstream asset should be inspected first. We address this gap by formulating pipeline root-cause analysis as a ranked retrieval problem, introducing a reproducible benchmark for open-source ETL/ELT incidents, and studying how runtime lineage, design lineage, and evidence signals can be fused under partial observability. Our contribution is therefore not another lineage collector, but a benchmarked RCA framework for a setting that remains underexplored in current data-engineering and provenance literature.
